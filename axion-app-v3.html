Quick reference

Strategy & Formula Sheet

A condensed reference for approach and recall — not a replacement for practice, but useful to skim before a study session or the night before test day.

Reading & Writing — approach

General approach
  • Read the question stem before you commit to reading the whole passage, form a rough prediction, then check it against the answer choices rather than reading all four cold.
  • Short passages reward precision over speed — skim past connective filler and pay closest attention to transitions and negation words ("but," "however," "not"), since they usually signal where the answer lives.
  • When a question asks you to fill in a blank or replace a word, restate the idea in your own words first, then match to the closest option.
Words in Context
  • Look for a synonym or antonym cue elsewhere in the sentence for the missing word.
  • Treat the blank as if the original wording weren't there — don't anchor to it.
  • Avoid choosing a word you don't recognize unless you've ruled out every other option first.
Central Ideas & Details
  • In nonfiction, the main idea usually sits near the opening or closing lines.
  • In fiction or narrative passages, the main idea tends to build gradually across the whole text rather than sitting in one line.
  • For detail questions, scan for the exact keyword from the question (or a close synonym) rather than re-reading everything.
Structure & Purpose
  • "Purpose" questions ask what a sentence or paragraph does, not just what it says — expect the correct answer to be phrased generally rather than tied to one specific detail.
  • For an underlined phrase, judge its function relative to the sentence right before and after it.
Inference & Evidence
  • For inference questions, expect to read the full passage rather than one line — inferences are built from context, not a single sentence.
  • For evidence questions, state the claim in plain words first, then check whether the graphic or textual evidence actually supports that specific claim (not just the general topic).
Cross-Text Connections
  • Identify the first text's point of view, then re-read the second text as if you held that same opinion — this makes agreement/disagreement questions much faster to answer.
  • When asked what both texts would agree on, look for overlapping specific details, not just a shared general topic.
Expression of Ideas
  • For transitions: read the sentence before and after the blank, name the logical relationship (contrast, cause-effect, addition, example), then pick the word that matches — don't just plug in what "sounds fine."
  • For rhetorical synthesis (combining bullet points): identify exactly what the question asks you to accomplish, then eliminate any choice that does something else — adds a comparison when none was asked for, states a conclusion when a purpose was asked for, etc.
Standard English Conventions
  • Every complete sentence needs a subject and a verb, and cannot start with only a dependent clause.
  • A comma alone cannot join two complete sentences (a comma splice) — that requires a period, semicolon, or a conjunction.
  • Match subject and verb number even when other words sit in between them; check tense consistency across a sentence unless context signals a real time shift.
  • A modifying phrase before a comma must describe whatever comes immediately after the comma.
  • Semicolons function like periods most of the time; colons must follow a complete sentence and typically introduce a list, example, or explanation; dashes can set off extra information or take the place of a comma, colon, or semicolon.
Common wrong-answer patterns
  • Watch for choices that are too broad, too narrow, off-topic, state the opposite of the correct meaning, use extreme/absolute wording, or slightly distort a true detail.

Percentages & ratios
  • Percent change = (new − original) / original.
  • To increase by a percent, multiply by (1 + decimal form); to decrease, multiply by (1 − decimal form).
  • A proportion a/b = c/d can be solved by cross-multiplying: ad = bc.
  • Direct variation: y = kx (both increase together); inverse variation: xy = k (one increases as the other decreases).

Statistics & data
  • Mean = sum ÷ count; median = middle value when ordered; mode = most frequent value.
  • Range = max − min; interquartile range (IQR) = Q3 − Q1, covering the middle 50% of the data.
  • Larger standard deviation means values are more spread out; smaller means they cluster near the mean.
  • Right-skewed data (long tail to the right) → mean is pulled above the median; left-skewed → mean is pulled below the median.
  • Be skeptical of answer choices using absolute language ("proves," "always," "never") — correct SAT answers about data usually use hedged wording like "suggests" or "indicates."
Probability
  • Probability = favorable outcomes ÷ total outcomes.
  • "And" (both events happen) → multiply probabilities if independent.
  • "Or" (either event happens) → add probabilities, then subtract any overlap if the events aren't mutually exclusive.
  • Without replacement, the total outcomes shrink for the second event — don't treat dependent events as independent.
